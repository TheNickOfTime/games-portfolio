Farternity

NickSeptember 2, 2018November 5, 2018

I just finished participating in the GMTK 2018 game jam. I worked with my good friends Garry and Elijah during this jam! With the prompt of making a game of a particular genre, but lacking one of the core features of that genre, the three of us came up with some wacky ideas. Our game concept was a stealth game with nowhere to hide. The premise ended up being that you are a dude at a party, but you constantly need to fart.

You can’t hide farts, so you have to cover them up in plain sight. Using distractions such as a smelly trash can, or a loud speaker, you have to methodically cover up your farts of different qualities such as smelly and silent, or odorless but loud.

Overall this was a very fun experience and my friends and I made a dumb little game that we can be proud of.

Notline Miami

NickDecember 21, 2017March 22, 2018

Notline Miami was the final project for two of my classes this semester. My goal was to combine the requirements of my UI scripting class and my gameplay scripting class to make a more complete game. Ultimately I wanted to create something that heavily mimicked the style of Hotline Miami (hence the name).

For the gameplay, I wanted a few things. Namely guns, enemies, and the objective which is to just kill everyone. The gun system was really fun to make and ended up being quite complex. I am very happy with how it turned out. That being said I wish I had time to implement melee weapons. The enemy AI was quite difficult to make, as all AI is. It is far from perfect, and this is probably the most choppy part of the project, but at the end of the day, AI is not my focus as a designer. The guns, enemy, and player come together to create a gameplay experience that is more like Hotline Miami than it is not, and I am happy about this. Given the period I had to work on this (~2 weeks) I am quite happy with the end result.

The UI side of this is probably what I enjoyed the most. On the functionality side of things, I enjoy scripting for UI more interesting and fun than other focuses (ahem… AI). Something great that I have taken away is a handful of scripts that I wrote that make certain generic UI tasks essentially drag and drop, no unique code required. These scripts will be a great foundation for the UI in my future projects. On the visual side of things for the UI, I really wanted to nail the aesthetic of the game. If it were up to me, I would have made it as close to 1:1 as possible, but I had certain grading requirements that forced me to deviate from the source. A great example of this would be the process of respawning. In the Hotline Miami it simply tells you to press ‘R’ to respawn, but I had to have a dedicated screen with several other buttons on this ‘Game Over’ screen. Overall I think that I nailed the style of UI in Hotline Miami. The funny thing is that I didn’t need to do this at all, as visuals were not a grading point of the assignment, but I figured if I was going to do it at all, I may as well do it right. While I got no extra points for the visual aspects of the project, I definitely learned a lot of things in the process, namely certain UI design principles, the importance of animations, motion, and use of color. I am not an artist, but these skills are important in many aspects of design.

Ultimately, I am very happy with this project. It is by far the most complicated code I have written to this point, and in the process, I learned a lot. Unfortunately, aside from the UI, design became something of an afterthought, as I became so absorbed into the technical side of things. Because of this, I cannot see this becoming a portfolio piece, but it will have a special place with this post, as well as in my heart. I have linked the playable build and Github page below!

Jumpman

NickNovember 30, 2017March 22, 2018

I was tasked with taking a mechanic from any game and replicating it at its most basic level of functionality, where it is still fun. Having just played Super Mario Odyssey, I was interested in replicating some of Mario’s movement mechanics. I did not have the time to replicate the signature ‘Cappy throw’ from the game, but I was able to replicate his famous triple jump, crouch jump, ground pound, and ground pound jump. From a design perspective, these moves all have unique situations where they aid in Mario’s mobility. This was a fun project that forced me to simplify the tech side of things and focus on fine tuning numbers on the design side of things to get it feeling just right. While there is room for improvement, I am quite happy with completing this in just a few hours, as I changed my mind on this assignment last minute. I recommend playing it, it is actually a fun little sample level to jump around in!

Space n’ Shit

NickNovember 15, 2017March 22, 2018

This is another 2 week design project. The goal was to create a twin-stick-shooter type game that had some sort of twist in the gameplay and ‘story’ of it. The story side of things is that you play as a small star who wants to become a big star. You float around outer space, picking up colorful bits of space dust, growing bigger and bigger, until you become so big that you implode and become a black hole. On the gameplay side of things, rather than shooting projectiles, the player controls a large solar flare that the star shoots out. This is primarily used as a tentacle of sorts to gather in more bits of space dust but is also your first defense against other stars and planets that come crashing at you, once you gain a large enough gravitational force. Creating the solar flare was a challenge, but ultimately the time was worth it, as it feels very nice to control and adds a ton of novelty to playing this minigame. Overall this was a fun project that ended up being a good split of design and technical focus.

Time: Part 1

Another 2-week project, but posting about 2 weeks late. Our objective was to make a game based on time. This prototype features a very rapid day/night cycle, borrowing some stuff from my “Summer Laziness” experiment. During the day it is blazing hot, and the player can only walk on shadows. Pac Man: Part 3

NickOctober 20, 2017October 20, 2017

I suppose I will never be done working on this project. Since the last update, I have done quite a few things. The most noticeable difference is that the enemy AI now does some rudimentary, custom pathfinding towards the player, making gameplay more challenging and interesting. Additionally, I rewrote the player and enemy controllers to derive from the same master controller class, I have been wanting to do this for a while now. Other than being easier to work with, it makes the implementation of a rewind time mechanic a lot simpler. The rewind time mechanic is also a new feature, and I will be working on it for the next ~2 weeks. Its functional, but there is a lot more that it could be doing.
